This document is a two-part internal operational guide for Team Inclan (Clemente Inclan’s team: primary attorneys, paralegals, legal assistants, runners, and junior attorneys) that sets concrete timelines, task ownership, and communication standards for defending cases in 2026. Email (1) is a “case checklist addendum” that converts the broader Expectation Guide into a day-by-day/phase-by-phase production schedule (first 10 days, 15–30 days, 15–60 days, 60–90 days, and post–90 days), assigning specific deliverables to the Primary attorney, paralegal, and legal assistant, with frequent hard deadlines (e.g., 24 hours, 5 days, 10 days, 30 days, day 65/75, 90 days). Email (2) is the longer Expectation Guide that sets the “how” (service philosophy, communication norms, Filevine uniformity, reporting cadence, discovery/records/chrono standards, trial-prep ramp) and reinforces deadline discipline.

Hello everyone,

Below I have created a checklist of how and when things should be completed in our cases consistent with the Expectation Guide I sent earlier today . The Expectation Guide notes when things should be completed but it also incorporates theory and philosophy. My hope in creating a checklist is to have a short to the point checklist that we can easily turn to. We are constantly talking about strategies and deadlines in our cases but my hope is that by creating a checklist we can all be on the same page all the time as we move forward in 2026. The goal here is to provide outstanding service to our clients.

(1.1.) Timeline – The First 10 days from the day of assignment – We need to get on the right foot immediately once a new case comes in. We only get one or two new cases a month: if that. But when they do come in, we need to make them a priority in so far as to get them moving in the right direction. As noted in the Team Inclan Expectation Guide; we need to prepare and submit to our clients a very good evaluation of a new case within 90 days of acceptance of an assignment: if not sooner. I believe that industry standard is 90 days from assignment to an initial comprehensive evaluation. We should aspire to have this even quicker: 60 to 90 days. It is the Primary lawyer in the case and the paralegal who bear the responsibility for this to happen. This is the first step towards excellence in the performance of our duties. In this timeline I recognize that I am front loading a lot to be done within the first 90 days of receiving a case and doing some of this may be difficult when trying to complete tasks on our older cases and or preparing or going to trial. This is especially difficult when the things we are trying to finish on our older cases are already overdue. We need to make a big push in the next 30 to 60 days to get everything caught up in cases. This may take some sacrifice but once we are caught up then it just be a matter of staying ahead. Getting things caught up is a necessity.

When a new case starts on the right track then there are less emergencies down the road. timelines and expectations noted herein and on the Team Inclan Expectation Guide are things that are industry standards and things I see other firms do. When I see other firms do these things, Again the I know they are on top of their cases. Most importantly, the adjuster sees that we are on top of things.

From time to time we do receive GL and trucking cases that are not in litigation. We should tackle those matters with urgency as well and generally take the same timeline approaches below as relevant or as otherwise take action as the pre-lit cases require:

(1.1.5.) 5. After initial meeting with insured – We need to send the insured a letter on letterhead confirming our representation, our role and attach the statement of insured’s right. The statement of insured’s rights is something that I forget to do at times and it is important that we provide same to the insured as per the bar guidelines. I think these statements confuse insureds and scare them as to what is going on because the statement says to the insured that they can get their own attorney. Insured often ask me why are you sending me something telling me to get an attorney when “I thought you are my attorney”. I have had multiple insureds ask me what is going on when they get these if I don’t preemptively let them know what it is. I always work really hard trying to establish trust with the insureds during our first meeting and of course throughout the pendency of the case. Meaning I want them to know that we are here for them and that they not the insurance company is our client. I spend a lot of time in the first meeting with insureds and later through the pendency of the case reminding them that while we are here for them, they still can hire their own attorney. Please note that the bar requires these letters to be in our file.

That is why it is important that when we meet with the insured initially we explain to them that we will be sending them a letter confirming our involvement and that said letter will be coming with the statement of insured’s right and what it means. It is important to preemptively allay any concerns that these statements may cause an insured.

For example, in a long-term care case, the Plaintiff may be critical that the Plaintiff fell, developed pneumonia and developed a pressure ulcer. The allegations need to be separately analyzed. Then we need to either confirm that the evidence is correct and or state the basis why the evidence is incorrect identifying what evidence is in support of the defense position. This sort of process should occur no matter what type of case it is.
